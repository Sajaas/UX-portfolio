Case Study

Construct & Combat

Construct & Combat is a game that teaches about the consequence of investing in warfare for casual gamers that enjoy simple strategy games.

It solves the problem of teaching about war by making the users reflect on their actions and engaging them by playing.

My role was to facilitate, document, prototype, test, and design the game and visuals. I collaborated with four other students.

The Problem
  • Challenge: create a tabletop game that reflects the theme and features several game modes
  • Client: World Beyond War
  • Duration: 3 weeks
My Roles
  • Facilitating
  • Documenting
  • Prototyping
  • Testing
  • Game & Visual Design
Defining the problem
Theme

The effects to the people when governments invest in warfare instead of infrastructure, and vice versa.

Requirements
  • 1-4 players
  • Feature PvP, PvE, solo, and Co-op modes
  • 25-30 mins per game
  • Easy rules & setup, 10-15 min onboarding
  • Engaging and replayable
  • Carry learning points
  • Playable online
  • Print & Play files
  • Sell sheet
  • Reflect the theme assigned clearly
Target Group

Casual gamers that enjoy simple strategy games, since the client wanted a game that would be used as a teaching tool in different settings.

Research
The Cost of War & Win Conditions

Researching and translating into possible gameplay. After researching, we defined a goal for the game (to make your citizens happy by building), and thought of ways to motivate the player.

Ideating & Prototyping
Game Setting

Ideated and voted on 2 top concepts. Before proceeding to the details of the game, we tested the setting and larger ideas on another group. This gave us the insight that our secondly prioritized game idea was more engaging, and we changed direction.

Game Format & Gameplay

Card game to make it simple. I suggested that we aim for a card game to make it portable, easier to incorporate the requirements for game modes, and less overwhelming to a new player. The team agreed and we ideated about win conditions and mechanics.

First Prototype

Easily modified prototype. Once we had decided on a few mechanics and gameplay modes, it was time to prototype to validate our assumptions. I found Playingcards.io which worked very well, and since we were going to iterate on the prototype I created an Excel sheet that I could simply import to the site.

Testing

Target group testing provided insights into how we could make the game more engaging and easier to understand.

Playtesting & Implementation
Second Prototype

Adding the game to Tabletop Simulator, in order to give the game a real game feeling and create better testing.

Visual Design

Designing the card faces, rulebook & Print&Play. Since I had experience with Photoshop, Excel, and Tabletop Simulator I designed the card faces, imported all the information, and added the game to the Tabletop Simulator Workshop on Steam.

Testing

Continuous testing to isolate & handle problems. During the prototyping we continuously tested the mechanics and game feel on our target group, to make sure we didn't break the game.

Final Playtest

I lead the final playtesting session in an online board game community. Afterwards I fixed the smaller errors and documented the more complicated issues & suggestions.

Presentation & Feedback

Happy customers. We pitched our game idea to the representatives of World Beyond War, and they were impressed with our product presentation and happy that we fulfilled all the demands.

Key Takeaways
  • Adapt the language to get understood
  • Easy rules, inviting structure, and low entry barrier
  • Use Lightning Decision Jams more
  • Use Dot Voting more
  • Assign speaking time during discussions online
Future Iterations
  • Incorporate more advanced mechanics
  • Lower the randomness
  • Add a detailed narrative
  • Include ideas from the presentation
  • Address problems from the final playtesting session
