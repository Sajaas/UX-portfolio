Case Study

Construct & Combat

Construct & Combat is a game that teaches about the consequence of investing in warfare for casual gamers that enjoy simple strategy games.

It solves the problem of teaching about war by making the users reflect on their actions and engaging them by playing.

My role was to facilitate, document, prototype, test, and design the game and visuals. I collaborated with four other students.

The Problem
  • Challenge: create a tabletop game that reflects the theme and features several game modes
  • Client: World Beyond War
  • Duration: 3 weeks
My Roles
  • Facilitating
  • Documenting
  • Prototyping
  • Testing
  • Game & Visual Design
The Brief
Theme

The effects to the people when governments invest in warfare instead of infrastructure, and vice versa.

Requirements
  • 1-4 players
  • Feature PvP, PvE, solo, and Co-op modes
  • 25-30 mins per game
  • Easy rules & setup, 10-15 min onboarding
  • Engaging and replayable
  • Carry learning points
  • Playable online
  • Print & Play files
  • Sell sheet
  • Reflect the theme assigned clearly
Empathize & Define
  • Researching the cost of war
  • Defining a goal for the game
  • Defining target audience
  • Defining player motivations
  • Specified game theme ideas
  • Dot voted and picked 2 top contestants
  • Pitched to another group to validate assumptions
Defining the audience & goals

With such a broad subject it was important to scale down to an MVP. Since the client wanted a game that would be used as a teaching tool in different settings, we decided on the target audience: "casual gamers that enjoy simple strategy games".

Game Theme

Before proceeding to the details of the game, we tested the theme and larger ideas on another group. This gave us the insight that our secondly prioritized game idea was more engaging, and we changed direction.

Ideating & Prototyping
  • Deciding game format
  • Ideated about win condition and gameplay
  • Created a prototype on Playingcards.io
  • Added an Excel sheet with card abilities so we can export CSV files to simplify updating the cards
  • Tried out concepts on target group
Game Decisions

I suggested that we aim for a card game to make it portable, easier to incorporate the requirements for game modes, and less overwhelming to a new player. The team agreed and we ideated about win conditions and mechanics.

First Prototype

Once we had decided on a few mechanics and gameplay modes, it was time to prototype to validate our assumptions. I researched quickly and found Playingcards.io to be fast, easy, and accessible, so I suggested that we tried it out. It worked very well and since we were going to iterate on the prototype I created an Excel sheet that I could simply import to the site. That way every team member could access the data and edit the mechanics and card details.

Playtesting & Implementation
  • Meeting with client to verify our ideas
  • Continous testing to isolate & handle problems
  • Designed card face templates with variables to import the CSV files
  • Designed a play area in Tabletop Simulator and imported the assets
  • Designed the rulebook, cheat cards & Print&Play-documents
  • The rest of the team prepared the Sell Sheet and presentation
Visual Design and Second Prototype

We split up the workload and since I had experience with Photoshop, Excel, and Tabletop Simulator I designed the card faces, imported all the information, and added the game to the Tabletop Simulator Workshop on Steam. I also designed the rulebook and Print&Play documents.

Final Playtest

During the prototyping we continuously tested the mechanics and game feel. I lead the final playtesting session in an online board game community. Afterwards I fixed the smaller errors and documented the more complicated issues & suggestions.

Presentation & Feedback

We pitched our game idea to the representatives of World Beyond War, and they were impressed with our product presentation and happy that we fulfilled all the demands.

Key Takeaways
  • Adapt the language to get understood
  • Easy rules, inviting structure, and low entry barrier
  • Use Lightning Decision Jams more
  • Use Dot Voting more
  • Assign speaking time during discussions online
Future Iterations
  • Incorporate more advanced mechanics
  • Lower the randomness
  • Add a detailed narrative
  • Include ideas from the presentation
  • Address problems from the final playtesting session
